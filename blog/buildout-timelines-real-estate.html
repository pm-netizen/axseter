Low Voltage Infrastructure: The Hidden Variable in Site Buildout Timelines

Why low voltage is often the last 10% of work that takes 30% of the timeline—and how real estate teams opening 50+ locations per year are fixing it.

Axseter Team

January 2026

You've negotiated the lease. The GC is mobilized. Permits are in hand. The opening date is locked, and marketing has already started promoting the new location.

Then the low voltage work becomes a problem.

If this sounds familiar, you're not alone. For real estate teams managing aggressive expansion schedules, low voltage infrastructure—structured cabling, access control, surveillance, audio/visual—is consistently the wildcard that threatens timelines.

Why Low Voltage Creates Timeline Risk

Low voltage sits in an awkward spot in the construction sequence. It's not early-stage work like framing or MEP rough-in. It's not finish work like flooring or paint. It falls somewhere in between, which creates coordination challenges.

Sequencing dependencies. Cable pathways need to be roughed in before drywall, but terminations can't happen until walls are painted and furniture is placed. Access control ties into door hardware, which ties into the door schedule, which ties into frame delivery. Each dependency creates potential for delay.

Last-minute scope changes. Operations wants to add two more camera positions. IT needs an extra network drop in the back office. These late requests are normal—but they cascade through the low voltage scope in ways that aren't always visible until you're behind schedule.

Vendor availability. The GC's preferred low voltage sub may not be available during your window. Finding a qualified replacement in an unfamiliar market takes time you don't have.

Inspection bottlenecks. Low voltage work often requires separate inspections from fire alarm, security, and building departments. Coordinating these inspections—especially in jurisdictions with backlogs—can add days or weeks.

The Cost of a Delayed Opening

For enterprises opening dozens of locations per year, the math on delayed openings is brutal.

Consider a healthcare clinic with projected monthly revenue of $400,000. A two-week delay costs roughly $200,000 in lost revenue—plus the carrying costs on the lease, the sunk marketing spend, and the operational disruption of rescheduling staff.

Or a QSR location with $80,000 in monthly revenue. The delay might only cost $40,000 directly, but multiply that across 100 locations per year and you're looking at real money.

The point isn't that low voltage is expensive. The point is that low voltage delays are expensive—far more expensive than the infrastructure itself.

How High-Velocity Real Estate Teams Approach This

Organizations that consistently hit opening dates at scale have figured out a few things about low voltage.

They treat it as a program, not a series of projects. Rather than scoping and bidding low voltage work site-by-site, they establish master specifications and pricing that apply across the portfolio. This eliminates the procurement cycle from the critical path.

They have a dedicated partner, not a rotating vendor list. When the same team handles every location, they learn your standards, your quirks, your non-negotiables. The fifteenth location goes faster than the fifth because there's no learning curve.

They build low voltage into the construction schedule from day one. Not as an afterthought, but as a parallel workstream with its own milestones and accountability. The low voltage partner is in the preconstruction meeting, not getting a call after drywall is hung.

They standardize relentlessly. Same camera models. Same access control hardware. Same rack configuration. Standardization isn't about limiting options—it's about eliminating the decisions that slow projects down.

What to Require from Your Low Voltage Partner

If you're opening 10+ locations per year, here's what your low voltage relationship should look like:

National coverage with local execution. You need a partner who can deploy in any market you're entering, with installation teams who know local codes and inspection processes.

Preconstruction engagement. Your low voltage partner should be reviewing plans and identifying coordination issues before construction starts—not discovering them in the field.

Fixed pricing by location type. For standard buildouts, you should know the low voltage cost before you sign the lease. Not a range—a number.

Schedule commitments with teeth. If your partner says they'll be done by a certain date, what happens if they're not? The answer shouldn't be "nothing."

Single point of accountability. You shouldn't need to coordinate between a cabling vendor, a security vendor, and an A/V vendor. One partner, one relationship, one throat to choke.

The Buildout Timeline That Actually Works

Here's what a well-coordinated low voltage timeline looks like on a typical 8-week retail or healthcare buildout:

Week 1-2: Preconstruction coordination. Low voltage partner reviews drawings, confirms scope, identifies any long-lead items.

Week 3: Rough-in. Cable pathways, conduit, back boxes installed while walls are open.

Week 4-5: Cable pull. Structured cabling installed after MEP but before drywall finishing.

Week 6: Trim and termination. Devices mounted, cables terminated, racks built.

Week 7: Testing and commissioning. Everything verified working before furniture and equipment.

Week 8: Final walkthrough, documentation delivery, punch list resolution.

This timeline assumes one thing: that your low voltage partner has done this before, in this market, with this type of buildout. Experience compresses timelines. Inexperience expands them.

The Real Estate Advantage

For real estate teams, reliable low voltage execution creates strategic options. You can commit to aggressive opening dates with confidence. You can take advantage of favorable lease terms that require fast occupancy. You can tell operations "yes" when they want to accelerate a high-priority market.

That flexibility has value beyond any single project. It's the difference between a real estate function that enables growth and one that constrains it.
